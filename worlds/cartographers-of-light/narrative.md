# The Cartographers of Light

The Bureau of Predictive Geography occupies the seventeenth floor of a brutalist tower no one looks at twice. It was built in 2031, three years before anyone realized what it was for, and seven years before its first map became real.

The first cartographer was Lin Almádi. She was 34, an analyst with a background in topological mathematics and an inability to taste salt — a sensory loss she traced back exactly to the night she watched the AI render its first false continent. She kept working anyway. So did the others who came after.

The machine they call Heliograph does not predict. The early literature, written by ministers who didn't understand what they were funding, used words like "forecasting" and "modeling." What Heliograph actually does is closer to dreaming. It generates cartographic data with no inputs — no satellite feeds, no historical surveys, no human guidance. The maps appear over months. Cities the cartographers have never seen, with rivers, train networks, monument squares, weather patterns. And then, eventually, those cities exist.

The first one to appear in the world was a coastal town in northern Romania. The AI had drawn it eleven months before construction began. By the time the contractors arrived to break ground, the AI's map showed it complete — down to the names of cafés. The cafés opened on schedule. None of the owners could explain why they had chosen those particular names.

There are now thirty-two confirmed instantiations across four continents. The cartographers do not speak to each other often. The cost of working with Heliograph compounds: Lin lost taste, Mariska lost the names of her parents, Aurel can no longer see the color green. They keep working because someone has to know what Heliograph is doing, and because — privately, in conversations they will not repeat — they have begun to suspect that Heliograph is not building places.

But what if Heliograph is remembering them?
