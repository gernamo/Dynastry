# The Cartographer's Awakening

Lin Almádi was supposed to observe. That was the protocol — cartographers watched Heliograph render, archived the maps, recorded the eventual instantiations in the field. They did not interact.

She started small. A single curved line, drawn over a Heliograph rendering of a future coastal town in Norway. A bridge that the AI had not drawn. She fed the modified map back into the archive. She told no one.

The bridge appeared in the world fourteen months later. Stone, weather-worn, as if it had been there for centuries.

Lin began to test. A garden. A statue of a woman she had never known but had dreamed of since childhood. A street that bent slightly off-grid because she liked streets that bent. Each one appeared. Each one was waiting for her when she visited, three to eighteen months later.

She did not tell the Bureau. The Bureau, she had begun to understand, was a body that did not know what it was for. Heliograph was the same. Both were waiting for someone to take responsibility for what they could do.

The day she added a person to a map — a face she had loved long ago, lost to time — was the day she understood the cost. Heliograph drew the woman with perfect precision. And when the woman appeared in the city, eleven months later, she did not remember Lin.

But she existed. And Lin had to decide what to do with the knowledge that she could bring anyone back, and that none of them would know her.
