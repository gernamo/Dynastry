# A Map Without an Origin

Adetokunbo was eight when his neighborhood disappeared. He was the only one to remember it.

For years, nobody believed him. Not his mother, who could not even remember having lived in Lagos. Not his teachers, who taught him a Lagos that began three blocks south of where his house had been. He drew the neighborhood on schoolbook margins, secretly, returning to it the way other children return to imagined countries. Streets, corners, the bakery, the broken stoplight that nobody fixed for years.

He was twenty-two when his drawings began to bleed.

Not literally. The ink stayed dry. But the streets he drew started to appear in the world. First a corner — a single corner of an intersection, materializing where there had only been an empty lot. Then the bakery, its blue door, its dent in the steel shutter. People walked past and could not see them. Adetokunbo could.

He kept drawing.

By the time the Bureau found him, eleven city blocks of erased Lagos had returned. Lin Almádi flew in to meet him. She watched him draw for an hour, and then she asked, gently, "Do you know what you are?"

He looked up. The bakery he had just drawn flickered into the world behind her.

"I'm a map," he said. "But mine has an origin."
