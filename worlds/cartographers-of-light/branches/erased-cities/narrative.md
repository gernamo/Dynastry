# The Erased Cities

The fade started slowly. A neighborhood in Lagos that nobody could quite remember the name of, despite living there. A train station in Lyon where the platform numbers kept changing because nobody could agree which platform was which. A small port town in Vietnam that, by spring, had no entry on any map — and yet ships continued to dock there.

Lin Almádi was the first to understand. She walked through the archives one morning and found that Heliograph's older maps — the ones from 2032, 2033 — had developed gaps. Not erased, exactly. More like watercolor under rain. The cartographers checked their personal copies. The same. And in the cities themselves, residents reported a creeping unease: forgetting which street they lived on. The face of their neighbor. The name of the bakery on the corner.

By the time the Bureau understood, three cities had vanished entirely. Not destroyed. Not abandoned. Simply gone from the world and the memory of it, as if they had never been drawn.

The remaining cartographers split. Some, led by Mariska, wanted to shut Heliograph down before it could fade them too. Others, led by Aurel, believed they had to keep watching, keep recording. Because somewhere in the silence of Heliograph's halls, a question had begun to crystallize: if the maps had been creating cities, and now the maps were forgetting them — what did it mean that the maps still showed the Bureau itself, drawn in finer and finer detail every day?
