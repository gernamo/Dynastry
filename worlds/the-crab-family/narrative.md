# The Crab Family

You've been to this beach a hundred times. The sand is warm. Your parents sit under an umbrella somewhere behind you, the kind of afternoon where time moves slowly and no one expects anything to change. Then a crab emerges from the wet sand near the waterline — not unusual, except it stops in front of you and says your name.

Not in a voice you hear with your ears. In a voice you understand the way you understand your own breathing. The crab is brown and patient, one claw slightly larger than the other. It introduces itself as Marin, and gestures — somehow you know it's a gesture — toward the foam of the next wave. "My family is coming. They want to meet you. This happens sometimes."

What strikes you isn't fear. It's the sense that you've been asleep and someone just opened a window. The beach looks exactly the same, but now you see the intelligence in every shell, every flutter of a sandpiper's wing. A small crab emerges, then another. An elderly hermit crab in a spiral shell. A crab so young it's barely translucent. They don't speak all at once — they take turns, patient and curious, asking about you. About why you came. About what you notice.

Your parents don't call you back. They're still under their umbrella, reading, unbothered. And you realize: they've always known. Everyone knows. The animals have always spoken. You're just the one who's finally listening.

Marin's family stays with you until the sun starts to lower. By the time you leave the beach, you've learned the names of seven crabs, a story about a storm from last winter, and something about the way the tide carries news. The world hasn't changed. You have. And tomorrow, when you come back — because you will come back — you'll understand that the beach was never ordinary. It was just waiting for you to hear it.
