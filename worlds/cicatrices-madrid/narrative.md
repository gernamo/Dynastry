# Cicatrices

He has spent twenty years learning the grammar of violence. Each scar on his body is a punctuation mark — a sentence he survived, a killer he found. His face is a map of the dead. Other detectives look away when he walks past. The brass keeps him because he closes cases, and because no killer wants to be caught by a man who understands them the way he does.

Madrid's northern neighborhoods are where money builds walls. Private schools, imported cars, gardens with security cameras pointing at nothing. The Parque de Bolas in Chamartín sits on a corner lot between a pediatrician's office and a tax attorney's building. On a Tuesday morning in May, a maintenance worker opens the pit to hose it down and finds a body tangled in the plastic balls. Female. Early twenties. The cuts are surgical.

By the time he arrives, the neighborhood is screaming. Parents are pulling their children from school. News vans are stacked like vultures. The wealthy do not tolerate chaos — they do not tolerate the suggestion that their children are not safe. The pressure from above will be enormous. But that is not why his chest tightens as he walks toward the pit.

It is the precision of it. The body is posed. The cuts do not rage; they deliberate. This is not a killer who loses control. This is someone who enters a children's play space, who knows exactly what he is doing, and who does it anyway. A different kind of animal. The scars on his body begin to itch — a phantom sensation, a warning his nervous system learned decades ago.

He has hunted men who kill in anger, in passion, in desperation. But a killer who murders in a place built for laughter, who chooses a ball pit as his canvas — that suggests a contempt so pure it borders on philosophy. And if that is what he is looking for, then the first body will not be the last.
