# The Discovery

Jimmy spent his days outside—in alleys, on fire escapes, in the spaces between buildings where the city forgot to finish itself. His hair was the color of rust and autumn. He moved like he owned the neighborhood, even though he owned nothing.

The basement door was half-hidden behind a dumpster, the kind of entrance that only exists if you're not looking for it. But Jimmy was always looking. The paint peeled in long strips—ochre bleeding into rust, rust into something cream-colored that might have been painted in 1987 or might have always been that color. The frame warped slightly when he looked at it straight on, as if the wood itself was breathing.

He pushed it open.

The hallway swallowed him. It was tall—impossibly tall—and full of things that had stopped being useful years ago. Telarañas hung like silk curtains. Dust motes turned in shafts of light that came from nowhere. The walls seemed to dissolve at the edges, their edges soft and imprecise like a watercolor left in the rain. His footsteps echoed twice, then disappeared into the walls themselves.

For a moment, Jimmy was small. Smaller than he'd ever felt. The space pressed down on him with the weight of all the years no one had been here.

Then something shifted. He wasn't small anymore. He was *inside* something that belonged to him. This hallway—this peeling, breathing, dissolving hallway—was waiting. Not for anyone. For *him*.

He turned and ran. Out of the basement, up the stairs, into the daylight. He had to tell them. He had to show them what he'd found. By the time he reached the street, he was shouting—histrionic, breathless, completely certain. The others would understand. They had to. Because Jimmy had just opened a door that would never really close again.
