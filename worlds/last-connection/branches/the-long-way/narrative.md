# The Long Way

He turned, and it was him.

And it was awful.

Not him — him was fine, him was a slightly shorter, more tired, more real person than the one she'd assembled from four-minute windows, and that was its own small adjustment. What was awful was the silence. On the train, six minutes of dead wifi had felt like held breath, like meaning. Standing three feet apart on a cold platform with full signal and nothing stopping them from talking forever, the silence was just silence. Enormous and ordinary and unbearable.

"Hi," he said.

"Hi," she said.

They stood there. Somewhere a vending machine hummed. Every brilliant thing they'd typed at 2 a.m. had evaporated, and what was left were two strangers who knew each other's deepest fears and could not, for the life of them, figure out where to get breakfast.

The four-minute magic was a function of the constraint. Take away the dead spots, the splitting train, the last stop racing toward them, and you take away the pressure that turned fragments into intimacy. They'd fallen for the format as much as each other. Now the format was gone, and underneath it were just two people who'd have to do the slow, unglamorous, terrifying work of actually knowing someone in real time — no six-minute window to draft the perfect reply, no last stop to make every word urgent.

They got coffee. It was bad coffee. The conversation lurched and stalled and occasionally, for a second or two, caught — a real laugh, a real silence that didn't hurt. He walked her to a bus she didn't need to take. She gave him a number that worked everywhere, not just on the 23:40.

It would take months. It would be nothing like the train. That was the point. Some things you have to learn the long way, from zero, in daylight, with all the time in the world and no excuse left to be brave.
