# Read at 3AM

Here is the thing nobody told them about the onboard chat: if you never close the thread, it stays. A ghost of a connection, parked in the corner of a messaging app, surviving long after the train and the night and the perfectly working router.

Three months later she couldn't sleep. She was scrolling the way you scroll at 3 a.m., through nothing, toward nothing, and there it was — the dead conversation from the train that meant nothing. *nice talking to you. you too.* She read it again. It had been so weightless. And yet she'd kept it. She'd never deleted it. She noticed that now, the way you notice a thing you've been pretending not to do.

She typed: *this is insane and you're definitely not going to see this. but I think the wifi worked too well that night. I think we'd have had a better chance if it kept dying.*

She sent it into what she was certain was a void.

The little word appeared under it. **Read.**

3:04 a.m. Three months later. He'd kept it open too.

The three dots started. Stopped. Started again — and she understood, with a jolt that woke her up completely, that he was sitting somewhere in the same sleepless dark doing exactly what she was doing: drafting, deleting, trying to make the four-minute version of himself in a window that, this time, would never run out. The constraint was gone. But maybe the constraint was never the point. Maybe it just taught them how to pay attention.

*I kept it open too,* he finally sent.

Then: *what stop are you near these days?*

This is the branch that gives the gaps back — not by breaking the signal, but by leaving a door open long enough for two people to walk back through it.
