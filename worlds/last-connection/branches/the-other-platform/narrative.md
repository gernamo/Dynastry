# The Other Platform

She didn't decide so much as find herself standing. The train was slowing for the junction, the chat was dying into another six-minute silence, and she had typed *I think I'm getting off at yours* and sent it into the dead window where it might not arrive for minutes, if at all.

Then she was on the platform. His platform. Cold concrete, a vending machine humming, four or five other passengers stepping down into the same blue pre-dawn. Any of them could be him. None of them looked up from their phones. She stood there with her backpack and her heart going stupid, watching strangers become ordinary people walking toward the exit.

Her phone buzzed. The platform had a signal the train never did. *you what??* Then, before she could answer: *which carriage did you get off* — and she realized she didn't know how to describe where she was except by what she could see, so she typed *vending machine. blue light. I'm the idiot who can't tell which one is you.*

A figure near the far end stopped walking.

What happens in the seconds after — whether he turns, whether the face matches anything she imagined, whether the person you've built out of four-minute fragments can survive becoming real — is the whole question this branch refuses to answer cleanly. Some leaps are about the landing. This one is about the half-second of air.

She had run from a life that stopped fitting. She had gotten off at the wrong platform on purpose. Whatever happened next, it would at least be hers.
