# Missed

The figure who stopped was a man checking whether he'd dropped his ticket. He hadn't. He kept walking.

He — the real him — was already through the exit by then, out on the street where the platform's signal didn't reach, phone in his pocket, walking the fifteen minutes home he'd walked a thousand times. Her messages stacked up behind a dead spot in the network, delivered all at once four minutes later to a screen he wasn't looking at.

She waited by the vending machine until the cold got into her properly. She typed *are you still here* and *I think I lost you* and finally, just *ok. that's ok.* and watched them sit there unread. When the next train rattled through without stopping, she understood. He was somewhere in this town, in a direction she couldn't guess, becoming again the stranger he'd been before 23:40.

He found the messages on his doorstep, key in the lock. Read them in order, the whole impossible thing — *I think I'm getting off at yours,* the vending machine, the blue light, the idiot who couldn't tell which one was him. He turned around. He went back. The platform was empty. The vending machine still hummed.

They were two people in the same small town at dawn, walking in opposite directions, each holding a phone with a conversation that had no more four-minute windows left. The local chat only worked on the train. Off it, they had no way to reach each other at all. They never had. That had been the whole strange grace of it — and now it was the whole cost.

Some connections are only ever last connections. This one just took the long way to admitting it.
