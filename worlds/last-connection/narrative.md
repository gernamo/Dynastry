# Last Connection

The 23:40 to the coast has wifi that works for exactly four minutes at a time, then dies for six. Everyone who takes this train knows it. You learn to send what matters in the four, and to sit with yourself in the six.

She boards at the central station with a backpack and no plan, running from a version of her life that stopped fitting. He boards two stops later, going home to something he isn't sure he wants to arrive at. They never see each other. The carriage is dark, half-empty, the kind of late where faces are just the blue rectangles lighting them from below.

It starts as an accident. The onboard network has a local chat — a relic nobody uses, meant for "passenger announcements." She types something into it out of boredom: *is anyone else's wifi completely useless or is it just me.* Six minutes of silence. Then, in the next four-minute window: *it's everyone's. that's the deal on this train. you pay in patience.*

So they talk in fragments. Four minutes on, six minutes off. The forced pauses do something strange — they make every message count, make the silences feel like held breath. In the gaps she watches the dark fields slide past. She imagines him three carriages up, or one seat behind, or not real at all. She never checks. Checking would end it.

They tell each other things you can only tell a stranger you'll never meet. Why she left. What he's going back to. The exact shape of the lives they're trying to outrun. By the third hour they've stopped pretending this is small talk. Somewhere around the halfway point he writes: *what's your stop?* and she stares at the question for a full dead-wifi cycle before answering.

Because here's the thing about the 23:40: it splits at the junction near the coast. Half the carriages go one way, half the other. Whoever gets off first disappears into a different morning, and the local chat doesn't follow you off the train. Whatever this is, it has a last stop. They both know it. Neither of them says it. They just keep typing into the four minutes, racing the map.

But what if one of them got off at the wrong platform on purpose?
